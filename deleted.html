Context
TIGP was my first real marketing role and a startup environment where there was no playbook. I joined as a marketing intern and ended up doing far more than I expected: content, sales outreach, client management and production support, often on the same day.
What I did
I was responsible for reaching out to potential clients, building relationships with leads and converting them into paying customers. In my second month, I converted a lead that brought in Rs 80,000 in revenue for the company. This came through consistent follow-up, understanding what the client actually needed, and communicating the value clearly.

Alongside this, I handled social media content planning and creation: building a calendar, writing captions and working on the brand's visual and tonal consistency across platforms. I also assisted on photoshoots including preparation, on-set coordination and support. The combination of sales, content and production gave me a hands-on view of how a small brand actually operates day to day.
"In a startup, there is no one to hand off to. You understand what a brand actually needs when you are doing the content, the outreach and the shoot prep at the same time, because they are all connected."
What I learned
Brand building in a startup is not about campaigns. It is about consistency, relationship and showing up. Every interaction with a lead is brand communication. Converting a client is not just a sales skill. It requires listening well enough to understand what someone actually needs before you offer them anything.

Context
During my time at 82.5 Communications, an Ogilvy agency, a significant part of my work involved competitor and communication scanning. I studied how brands across very different categories talk to consumers, what emotional spaces they occupy and where the gaps are.
What I did
Across multiple briefs, I studied brands including BKT Tyres, Pediasure, Franklin Templeton and IDBI Bank, along with their category competitors, across industries ranging from tyres and banking to health and nutrition and alcohol. A recurring focus was identifying which positioning spaces were already taken so the teams I supported could find fresh narrative territory.

I was part of an ideation session where I observed how senior strategists translate research into the foundations of a campaign. I also attended a pre-production meeting and a live shoot for Mother's Recipe, which changed how I understand the relationship between brand strategy and execution.
"Each brief taught me the same thing differently: the question is not what competitors are doing. It is what space they have left open."
What I learned
Competitor analysis is a strategic tool, not just a research task. I also learned to read communication tone and visual language as signals of brand positioning, which is something I now apply when looking at any brand's output.

Copywriting
Saying more with less: writing copy for Franklin Templeton and Mother's Recipe
82.5 Communications, Ogilvy · 2025 · Copywriting support
Social copyOutdoor copyConsumer insightFinancial servicesFMCG
expand ↓
Context
I had the opportunity to support the copywriting team on social media content for Franklin Templeton and on hoarding copy for Mother's Recipe. Two very different briefs, two very different disciplines.
What I contributed
For Franklin Templeton, I developed multiple candidate copy lines for social campaigns, working alongside a fellow intern. The brief required brevity, clarity and a tone that felt intelligent without being inaccessible. Lines were reviewed and shortlisted by the senior copywriting team.

For Mother's Recipe, I worked on hoarding copy: an entirely different challenge. Outdoor advertising gives you even less space and zero context. A person passing a hoarding at speed has about two seconds. Every word has to earn its place.
"Share your playlist, not your password."
"If it looks like bait, better wait."
"Suspicious link? Stop and think."
"Copy is research plus insight plus the ability to say more with less. I understood that only after writing lines that did not make the cut and figuring out why."
What I learned
Different media demand different copy thinking. Social copy can breathe a little. Outdoor copy cannot. Both start from the same place: a sharp consumer insight. But what you do with it depends entirely on where and how someone will encounter it.
Copy lines above are from my personal ideation for Franklin Templeton social campaigns. Shortlisting decisions were made by the senior team. Specific campaign details are confidential.

As part of a brief for Himalaya, I conducted primary consumer interviews to understand how people actually perceived and related to the brand, not what the brand assumed. The project
A structured research engagement for Naturell, a health and wellness brand. The goal was to understand in-store performance: how visible the brand was at the point of sale, how well-placed its products were relative to competitors and how consumers responded when they actually encountered the brand on the shelf.
What I did
I conducted in-store audits across 14 plus retail locations, studying SKU placement, visibility, shelf positioning and signage. I then gathered consumer feedback to surface the gap between what the brand intended and what shoppers actually perceived at the moment of purchase.
"A brand can have the right product in the wrong place. The audit taught me that where something sits on a shelf is as much a brand decision as the packaging or the campaign."
What I learned
Brand strategy does not end with the ad. It has to work at the point of purchase too. Retail is a media channel. This project gave me a grounded understanding of how brand perception is shaped by physical presence, not just communication.
